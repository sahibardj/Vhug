Menstrual Hygiene and Impact on Ecology.

  • Home
  • Blog

Plastic pollution is the accumulation of plastic waste, and our management system is failing us with each passing day. Landfills are overflowing, and it's high time we take a constructive step to reduce it to some extent. Plastic pervades modern lifestyle, and menstruation is no exception.

A healthy woman menstruates for about 35 to 40 years, accounting for 5 days a month, making it over 2100 days. If a woman uses regular sanitary pad throughout this time, making it over 7,200 pads when changed according to the prescribed time.

Regular sanitary pads are made up of 90% plastic, whereas tampons constitute 6% plastic (without applicator). Standard sanitary pads are opted because of it being expedited. If we account for even 30 percent of women, the waste being generated is oodles.

"I don't want to contribute 40 years of garbage to a landfill just to manage something that shouldn't even be seen as a problem,"- Ann Borowski, a researcher on the ecological impact of sanitary products. We can not eradicate plastic, but we, for sure, can reduce it to a great extent.

Though the plastic used in these products is of medical grade and does not need to be tracked down, it doesn't make it any better. Getting a hand on how much plastic waste comes from menstrual products is challenging because little research has looked into the problem's scope.

Researchers have been developing eco-friendly, biodegradable options to replace or minimize the use of regular pads due to this helping the environment to a great extent. Some of the options that create far less pollution than traditional pads and tampons are menstrual cups, biodegradable pads, reusable pads, and period pants. All the options listed above may come handy considering the waste generation.


arrow_forwardMenstrual cups are silicone cups, safe to use for 6-7 years or more. They can come handy as the waste generated will be far less. These are pocket friendly, so affordability is not a question.
